## Core Thesis

We are building a civilization increasingly dependent on software that no human understands — not because the technology is too advanced, but because we traded comprehension for velocity. This creates an unprecedented civilizational bus factor where the knowledge to maintain critical systems was never held by humans in the first place.

## Three Interlocking Arguments

### 1. The Deskilling Spiral (Individual Level)
Vibe coding produces working software faster — but each successful output erodes the developer's ability to work without AI. The better it works, the less you learn. Bainbridge's "Ironies of Automation" playing out in real-time.

### 2. The Civilizational Bus Factor (Systemic Level)
Traditional bus factor: people leave, knowledge leaves with them. New bus factor: knowledge was never in human heads. There's no fallback gradient. You can't "fall back" to manually understanding code nobody ever understood.

### 3. Clarke's Law Inverted (Philosophical Level)
Clarke imagined technology so advanced it *appears* magical. We're building technology we *could* understand but are choosing not to. The magic-makers don't understand their own magic. No wizards, only spells.

## Contrarian Positions to Address

1. **"This is just the next layer of abstraction"** — Assembly → C → Python → AI. Each transition had the same fears. Counterpoint: previous abstractions were deterministic and inspectable.

2. **"Collective understanding always existed"** — Nobody understands a CPU solo, but collectively we do. Counterpoint: with AI-generated code, even the collective understanding may not exist.

3. **"The AI *is* the understanding"** — The knowledge isn't lost, it's in the model. Counterpoint: the model is non-deterministic, version-dependent, and controlled by a third party.

4. **"Market forces will self-correct"** — If dependency becomes dangerous, people will learn to code again. Counterpoint: the return path gets harder with each step, and the skills may atrophy past recovery.
